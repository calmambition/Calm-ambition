5 Signs You're
Burning Out.
Not Just Tired.

Burnout doesn't announce itself with a dramatic collapse. It creeps in quietly, disguised as things that feel almost reasonable. Here are the five signs high performers most commonly overlook.

Calm Ambition · 6 min read

Everyone gets tired. Deadlines pass, weekends happen, you recover.

Burnout is different.

It doesn't look like what most people imagine. We picture a collapse. Someone falling apart. For most high performers it looks nothing like that. It looks like someone functioning well externally while quietly running on empty internally.

Which is to say: it looks like you.

The traits that got you here are exactly what make it hard to see. You're too good at pushing through.

1. You're exhausted, but sleep doesn't help

When you're simply tired, rest restores you. You sleep, you recover, you move on.

When you're burning out, sleep stops being enough. Even a good night's rest leaves you waking up already depleted. If you've been going to bed tired and waking up tired for weeks rather than days, that pattern is significant.

The nervous system has a finite capacity for sustained pressure. When it's been running on high alert for long enough, ordinary rest doesn't touch it.

2. The things that used to energise you no longer do

Think about the work that once genuinely excited you. The projects you'd have welcomed. The parts of your role that felt meaningful.

Now think about how those same things feel today.

If the answer is flat, or just another thing to get through, that shift matters. It often arrives so gradually that you don't notice the drift until the distance is already vast.

It doesn't mean you've lost your drive. It means your system is protecting itself.

3. You're productive but feel completely empty

You're still delivering. Outputs look fine. People around you have no idea. But internally there's a growing disconnection between what you're doing and any sense of meaning in doing it.

Going through the motions. Performing competently while feeling strangely absent from your own work.

Continuing to perform well is not evidence that things are fine. For high performers, it's often what makes burnout stay invisible longest.

4. Small things are hitting harder than they should

A snappy email. An interruption at the wrong moment. Things that wouldn't have touched you six months ago are now taking real effort to shake off.

High performers tend to have good composure. When that composure starts costing more to maintain, it's usually a signal worth heeding rather than suppressing.

5. You've lost access to the version of yourself you trust

This is perhaps the hardest one to name.

A sense that the capable, clear-headed version of yourself has become harder to reach. More uncertain than you used to be. Less decisive. Operating slightly below your own standard without quite knowing why.

This isn't weakness. It's what happens when the resources that sustain high performance are running genuinely low.

What to do if you recognise yourself here

The first step is also the hardest: acknowledging that what you're experiencing is real and significant. Not something to push through or wait out.

Burnout in high performers rarely resolves through better productivity systems or time management. The patterns that drive it go deeper than that. The relationship with pressure, with rest, with what you've been allowing to count as enough.

Left unaddressed, those patterns tend to compound. The distance from yourself grows. The cost increases.

If several of these feel familiar, the most useful thing isn't another tool. It's understanding what's actually driving what you're experiencing. And building a different way of working from the inside out.
