Cortisol
Isn't the
Enemy

The wellness industry has decided cortisol is a toxin. The research disagrees. What burnout actually does to cortisol, and why trying to lower it at the wrong stage makes recovery harder.

Priyanka · 24 June 2026 · 6 min read

You've seen the content. "High cortisol is ruining your health." "Signs your cortisol is too high." Supplements, detox protocols, before-and-afters. Cortisol has become the hormone wellness culture loves to demonise.

If you're in burnout or chronic stress, this framing points in the wrong direction. Understanding what cortisol actually does, and what burnout actually does to it, changes the intervention.

What cortisol actually does

Cortisol does not exist to harm you. It mobilises energy, regulates inflammation, supports immune function, sharpens memory under pressure, and keeps you functional in the face of demand. Without cortisol, the body cannot mount a basic stress response. People with Addison's disease, where cortisol production fails, cannot get through an ordinary day without supplementation.

The cortisol awakening response sets up your whole day. In the 30 to 45 minutes after waking, cortisol spikes to prepare the body for what's ahead: it raises blood sugar for energy, sharpens attention, and sets the metabolic tone for the hours that follow. When that spike is weak, you wake flat, slow, and unable to get started.

Cortisol then follows a diurnal curve: highest in the morning, declining through the afternoon, reaching its lowest point around midnight so melatonin can rise and the body can repair. It organises the body's energy and recovery cycles around the 24-hour day.

What burnout actually does to cortisol

Months of chronic stress dysregulate the pattern. The problem is timing, not a permanently sky-high level.

The morning spike flattens. The afternoon decline disappears. Cortisol that should have dropped by evening persists into the night, suppressing melatonin and cutting off the recovery window the body depends on.

In advanced burnout, the picture shifts further. A 2024 peer-reviewed study examining cortisol reactivity in physicians found that those with burnout produced a weaker cortisol response under acute stress than colleagues without burnout. The system had run depleted. The hormone that should spike when demands arrive no longer spikes. Researchers call this hypocortisolism: the HPA axis, after years of chronic activation, shifts into a low-output state.

The person in burnout who cannot get through a meeting, who has no drive before noon, who cannot respond proportionately to ordinary demands: their cortisol may be running too low, not too high.

Why "lower your cortisol" is the wrong target

The wellness industry builds its cortisol advice on one premise: chronic stress raises cortisol, elevated cortisol harms the body, therefore lower it. For early-stage stress, that holds.

An acute stressor raises cortisol, and if the stressor continues, cortisol stays elevated longer than it should. Slow breathing, regular movement, and recovery time support the parasympathetic nervous system and help the cortisol curve return toward its baseline. At this stage, the advice is sound.

By the time most people recognise they are burned out, they are past this stage. The HPA axis has already shifted. The diurnal curve has already flattened. Directing interventions at lowering cortisol in a depleted system targets the wrong problem.

What restores the pattern

Restoring cortisol rhythm requires the same anchors that restore any circadian disruption.

Morning light exposure strengthens the cortisol awakening response. Direct daylight in the first 30 minutes after waking is one of the most reliable inputs for re-establishing the diurnal curve. A consistent wake time gives the HPA axis a reliable signal to organise around. Both appear across the circadian burnout research as recovery-supporting practices.

Exercise supports healthy cortisol rhythmicity, but timing matters. Morning or early afternoon movement works with the natural curve. Hard training late in the evening raises cortisol at the wrong point in the cycle and can cut into the overnight recovery window.

Nutritional timing also plays a role. Skipping breakfast or very low-carbohydrate eating can suppress the cortisol awakening response in already-depleted individuals. The body needs fuel at the start of the day to support normal morning cortisol function.

Recovery targets a functioning pattern: cortisol peaking when it belongs, dropping when it should.

The cortisol detox trend treats a critical hormone as a toxin. The research treats it as a biomarker: a signal that shows where the timing system is and whether the HPA axis is functioning.

For most people in burnout, the cortisol problem is dysregulation: a pattern that lost its shape under sustained pressure and a system that ran depleted from managing without adequate recovery.

Knowing that changes what you do. Practices aimed at reducing cortisol in a depleted system miss the target. What helps is rebuilding the rhythm: the same anchors, held long enough for the curve to recover its shape.

That rebuild is slow, which is why burnout takes longer to recover from than most people expect.
